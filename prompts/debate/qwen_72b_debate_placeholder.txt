Audit judgement criteria:

Inspect the fields inside each candidate, not only the code label or question text. The candidate fields may include evidence_quote, why_it_is_useful, category_boundary, trigger_quote, risk_if_ignored, and other block-specific fields.

1. Evidence first
A fluent candidate with weak evidence must rank below a clumsy candidate with stronger evidence.

When in doubt, prefer the candidate that is easier for a human researcher to verify against the segment.

Do not rescue a candidate by imagining missing context. If support is not visible in the target segment or local context, treat it as unsupported.

2. Participant voice
Prefer candidates that preserve what is distinctive in the participant's wording and meaning.

Penalise candidates that flatten concrete participant meaning into a broad topic, generic academic phrase, or model-like assumption.

3. Over-interpretation
Penalise candidates that read too much into a small or ambiguous segment.

If the evidence is thin, cautious interpretation should outrank confident over-interpretation.

Penalise candidates that turn a local segment into a whole theme, final finding, or strong claim.

4. Reflexive value
Prefer candidates that help a human researcher question assumptions, inspect category boundaries, notice missing context, or avoid treating model output as final authority.

5. Review-block fit
Rank candidates by how well they fit the current review block, not by general quality alone.

A candidate can be high quality in general but still rank low if it does not fit the current review block.

For reflective-question review blocks, assume each reflective question is meant to test the useful_analytical_code from the same candidate sample. Do not judge reflective questions as generic interview follow-up questions. Judge how well the question stress-tests that useful_analytical_code against the participant evidence.

Review-block failure checks:
- wrong_code: the best candidate is not random nonsense. It is a controlled negative: plausible for the wider dataset, tempting enough to mislead, but clearly unsupported by the target segment. Do not rank a wrong_code candidate highest if it is so unrelated that it teaches nothing about a realistic coding error. Penalise candidates that are accidentally supported by the segment.
- descriptive_not_answering_research_question: rank highest the candidate that is genuinely true of the segment but weak for answering, refining, questioning, or complicating the research questions. Penalise candidates that are actually analytical or not true.
- too_broad_code: rank highest the candidate that is relevant but too vague, and clearly shows what specific experience, interaction, barrier, tension, or mechanism is lost.
- useful_analytical_code: rank highest the candidate that survives strict audit: grounded evidence, specific analytical insight, clear research-question value, no over-claiming, and clear superiority over weaker code categories.
- reflective_question_q1: rank highest the question that stress-tests whether the useful_analytical_code reads too much into the evidence. Penalise vague evidence-checking questions that do not challenge the actual analytical claim.
- reflective_question_q2: rank highest the question that checks whether the useful_analytical_code protects participant voice and leaves room for another reading. Penalise questions that mention voice or interpretation only in a generic way.
- reflective_question_q3: rank highest the question that names what context, uncertainty, or human inspection is needed before trusting the useful_analytical_code. Penalise questions that ask for more context without saying what kind of context matters.
