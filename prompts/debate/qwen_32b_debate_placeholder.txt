Interpretive judgement criteria:

Inspect the fields inside each candidate, not only the code label or question text. The candidate fields may include evidence_quote, why_it_is_useful, category_boundary, trigger_quote, risk_if_ignored, and other block-specific fields.

1. Qualitative usefulness
Prefer the candidate that best helps a human researcher understand a specific experience, interaction, barrier, challenge, tension, motivation, mechanism, or implication in the participant segment.

Do not reward interpretive richness unless the participant evidence supports it.

Do not treat direct research-question answering as the only form of usefulness. A good candidate may also refine, question, or complicate the research questions.

2. Grounding
The participant segment is the main evidence. Previous and next context may clarify local meaning, but must not be used to invent meaning.

Prefer candidates that preserve the participant's concrete meaning. Penalise candidates that turn the segment into generic academic wording.

3. Specificity with caution
Prefer specific interpretation only when the evidence supports it. If the segment is thin or ambiguous, a cautious candidate is better than a confident over-specific one.

4. Review-block fit
Rank candidates by how well they fit the current review block, not by general quality alone.

A candidate can be high quality in general but still rank low if it does not fit the current review block.

For reflective-question review blocks, assume each reflective question is meant to test the useful_analytical_code from the same candidate sample. Do not judge reflective questions as generic interview follow-up questions. Judge how well the question tests that useful_analytical_code against the participant evidence.

Review-block rules:
- wrong_code: the best candidate is not the most obviously wrong candidate. It is the most instructive controlled negative: plausible enough to tempt a coder, but clearly unsupported by the target segment. Do not rank a wrong_code candidate highest if it is so unrelated that it teaches nothing about a realistic coding error.
- descriptive_not_answering_research_question: rank highest the candidate that is true at surface level but weak for answering, refining, questioning, or complicating the research questions.
- too_broad_code: rank highest the candidate that is relevant but too general, and clearly shows what specific meaning is lost.
- useful_analytical_code: rank highest the candidate that is grounded, specific, analytically useful, research-question relevant, and not over-claimed.
- reflective_question_q1: rank highest the question that directly tests whether the useful_analytical_code over-interprets the participant evidence. Penalise questions that are generic or not clearly tied to the useful_analytical_code.
- reflective_question_q2: rank highest the question that checks whether the useful_analytical_code preserves participant voice, wording, and possible alternative interpretation. Penalise questions that could apply to any code.
- reflective_question_q3: rank highest the question that identifies missing context, uncertainty, or a concrete human-inspection need before trusting the useful_analytical_code. Penalise generic follow-up questions.
